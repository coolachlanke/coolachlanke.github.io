Radius of Curvature

This article chronicles my journey investigating the radius of curvature for univariate and parametric curves (including 3D). We’ll derive the formulas, visualize the concepts, and see where this knowledge can be applied, including (perhaps) the design of screw threads.

1. Introduction & Motivation

Curvature describes how sharply a curve bends, and the radius of curvature (ROC) is the reciprocal of the curvature at a given point. Understanding ROC helps in geometric design, computer graphics, and physics.

My inspiration for this project was a random note about measuring the “bending” of a 2D function. This led me down a rabbit hole: from geometry class definitions, to calculus derivations, to verifying it all with parametric and 3D curves.

2. Radius of Curvature for Univariate Functions

Let’s start with a classic function \( y = f(x) \). The curvature \(\kappa\) at a point is given by:

$$ \kappa(x) = \frac{|y''(x)|}{\bigl(1 + (y'(x))^2 \bigr)^{3/2}} $$

Hence, the radius of curvature \(R(x)\) is:

$$ R(x) = \frac{1}{\kappa(x)} = \frac{\bigl(1 + (y'(x))^2 \bigr)^{3/2}}{|y''(x)|}. $$

In deriving this, we look at the geometry of the osculating circle—the circle that “best fits” the curve locally.

3. Radius of Curvature for 2D Parametric Curves

When a curve is parameterized by \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) in 2D, the curvature becomes:

$$ \kappa(t) = \frac{\bigl| x'(t) y''(t) - y'(t) x''(t) \bigr|} {\bigl(\bigl(x'(t)\bigr)^2 + \bigl(y'(t)\bigr)^2\bigr)^{3/2}}. $$

And so the radius of curvature is simply:

$$ R(t) = \frac{1}{\kappa(t)}. $$

This form of the equation is particularly handy when dealing with circles, ellipses, or other shapes where a natural parameterization (like angle or time) may be more convenient than just \(y=f(x)\).

4. Radius of Curvature for 3D Parametric Curves

In 3D, for a curve given by \(\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t), z(t) \rangle\), the curvature \(\kappa\) is:

$$ \kappa(t) = \frac{\|\mathbf{r}'(t) \times \mathbf{r}''(t)\|} {\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|^3}. $$

As before, \(R(t) = 1/\kappa(t)\). Geometrically, this is tied to the concept of the osculating circle that lies in the osculating plane at each point of the 3D curve.

The cross-product in the numerator indicates that if the velocity and acceleration vectors are collinear (i.e., the motion is in a straight line), curvature is zero (infinite radius of curvature).

6. Application: Screw Thread Design

One practical application of ROC is in the design and manufacturing of screw threads. Screw threads can be modeled with helical curves, which are 3D parametric curves:

$$ x(t) = r \cos(t), \quad y(t) = r \sin(t), \quad z(t) = \frac{h}{2\pi} \, t, $$

where \(r\) is the radius of the helix, and \(h\) is the pitch height per \(2\pi\) radians. By analyzing curvature (and torsion), we gain insight into how “steeply” the screw angle is cut, or how the thread might wear over time.

In many industrial settings, controlling the radius of curvature ensures that stress and wear patterns on the thread are maintained within safe limits.

7. Conclusion

Through exploring univariate, 2D, and 3D formulations of curvature and radius of curvature, we can appreciate the central importance of this geometric measure. It drives design considerations in mechanical engineering, computer graphics, and beyond.

The relationships between derivatives and curvature provide a powerful lens on how “bendy” shapes are, and hopefully, this page demonstrated just how straightforward and elegant the math behind curvature can be!

If you want to dive deeper, I encourage you to check out the embedded Desmos graphs, or even replicate these calculations in your own Python or MATLAB code to verify how the radius of curvature behaves for different test curves.
